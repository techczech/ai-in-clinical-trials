## 2. Key Areas of Utility (Where GenAI is Most Useful Now)

The report identifies four primary domains where GenAI is currently demonstrating value:

### 2.1 Trial Design and Administration
*   **Document Drafting:** LLMs can draft protocol sections, informed consent forms, and study reports, significantly reducing development time and administrative burden.
*   **Policy & Regulatory Optimization:** Proposals suggest regulators could use LLMs trained on past successful/failed protocols to automatically flag design flaws, predict approval chances, and optimize eligibility criteria.
*   **Hypothesis & Protocol Refinement:** Tools are being piloted for hypothesis generation, sample-size calculation, and protocol refinement.
*   **Key Citations:** Foote et al., 2025; Prabhakar, 2024; Sevier et al., 2025; Liddicoat et al., 2025; Fleurence et al., 2024.

### 2.2 Recruitment, Eligibility, and Personalization
*   **Patient-Trial Matching:** AI (including LLM-based TrialGPT) can match patients to trials with ~87% accuracy, >90% recall, and ~40% reduction in screening time.
*   **Outcome Prediction:** Reviews in oncology and broader trials show AI improves enrollment, outcome prediction, and personalized treatment strategies.
*   **Technology Shift:** Mainly via ML but increasingly using generative models and LLMs.
*   **Key Citations:** Mansoor & Ebrahim, 2025; Cascini et al., 2022; Saady et al., 2025; Askin et al., 2023; Reddy, 2024.

### 2.3 Simulation, Digital Twins, and Synthetic Data
*   **Control Arm Simulation:** Generative models can simulate placebo/control arms and patient trajectories, potentially increasing power, reducing sample sizes, and shortening trials while lowering ethical burdens.
*   **Synthetic Patients:** GAN-based synthetic patients enable data sharing and scenario testing, with prospects for richer digital twin-based *in silico* trials.
*   **Key Citations:** Bordukova et al., 2023; Foote et al., 2025; Vidovszky et al., 2024.

### 2.4 Risk, Monitoring, and Health Technology Assessment (HTA)
*   **Risk-Based Monitoring:** GenAI supports trial-risk prediction, adaptive designs, and risk-based monitoring within broader AI frameworks.
*   **Evidence Synthesis:** For health technology assessment, it can streamline evidence synthesis and trial data analysis.
*   **Caveat:** Remains experimental and requires strong human oversight.
*   **Key Citations:** Mansoor & Ebrahim, 2025; Teodoro et al., 2025; Shanmugam et al., 2025; Fleurence et al., 2024.

## 4. Unresolved Questions ("What's Still Unclear")

The report explicitly calls out critical gaps:
*   **Lack of Prospective Evidence:** Very few prospective randomized evaluations of generative AI as a trial-process tool exist; most evidence is conceptual, retrospective, or in pilots.
*   **Evolving Governance:** Regulatory pathways, liability, bias mitigation, and data governance frameworks are still evolving and may constrain high-stakes uses (e.g., endpoint adjudication, primary analyses).

---

## 5. Conclusion

Generative AI is already useful for:
*   **Administrative automation**
*   **Protocol support**
*   **Recruitment optimization**
*   **Simulation**

...and could meaningfully accelerate and de-risk clinical trials.

**However, its most transformative uses (digital twins, AI-designed adaptive trials) remain largely aspirational and require:**
1.  Rigorous prospective evaluation.
2.  Robust regulation.
3.  Strong human oversight.
