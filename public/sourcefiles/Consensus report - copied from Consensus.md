# **Generative AI in Clinical Trials:** High Potential, Early Evidence, Significant Caveats

Generative AI appears **highly useful for making clinical trials faster, cheaper, and more inclusive**, but current evidence is mostly early-stage, with few prospective, regulated deployments.

## Where generative AI is most useful now

### 1. Trial design and administration  
- Large language models (LLMs) can draft protocol sections, informed consent forms, and study reports, reducing development time and administrative burden  (Foote et al., 2025; Prabhakar, 2024; Sevier et al., 2025).  
- Policy proposals for application‑specific LLMs suggest regulators could use past successful/failed protocols to automatically flag design flaws, predict approval chances, and optimize eligibility criteria  (Liddicoat et al., 2025; Fleurence et al., 2024).  
- Generative AI–driven tools are being piloted for hypothesis generation, sample‑size calculation, and protocol refinement  (Prabhakar, 2024; Sevier et al., 2025).  

### 2. Recruitment, eligibility, and personalization  
- AI (including LLM-based TrialGPT) can match patients to trials with ~87% accuracy, >90% recall, and ~40% reduction in screening time, suggesting large efficiency gains in recruitment  (Mansoor & Ebrahim, 2025; Cascini et al., 2022).  
- Reviews in oncology and broader trials show AI improves enrollment, outcome prediction, and personalized treatment strategies, mainly via ML but increasingly using generative models and LLMs  (Saady et al., 2025; Askin et al., 2023; Reddy, 2024).  

### 3. Simulation, digital twins, and synthetic data  
- Generative models can simulate placebo/control arms and patient trajectories, potentially increasing power, reducing sample sizes, and shortening trials while lowering ethical burdens  (Bordukova et al., 2023; Foote et al., 2025; Vidovszky et al., 2024).  
- GAN‑based synthetic patients enable data sharing and scenario testing, with prospects for richer digital twin–based in silico trials  (Bordukova et al., 2023; Vidovszky et al., 2024).  

### 4. Risk, monitoring, and HTA  
- Generative AI supports trial‑risk prediction, adaptive designs, and risk‑based monitoring within broader AI frameworks  (Mansoor & Ebrahim, 2025; Teodoro et al., 2025).  
- For health technology assessment, it can streamline evidence synthesis and trial data analysis, but remains experimental and requires strong human oversight  (Shanmugam et al., 2025; Fleurence et al., 2024).

## What’s still unclear

- Very few prospective randomized evaluations of generative AI as a trial‑process tool exist; most evidence is conceptual, retrospective, or in pilots  (Foote et al., 2025; Han et al., 2024; Teodoro et al., 2025).  
- Regulatory pathways, liability, bias mitigation, and data governance frameworks are still evolving and may constrain high‑stakes uses (e.g., endpoint adjudication, primary analyses)  (Liddicoat et al., 2025; Foote et al., 2025; Fleurence et al., 2024; Reddy, 2024).  

## Conclusion

Generative AI is already useful for **administrative automation, protocol support, recruitment optimization, and simulation**, and could meaningfully accelerate and de‑risk clinical trials. However, its most transformative uses (digital twins, AI‑designed adaptive trials) remain largely aspirational and require rigorous prospective evaluation, robust regulation, and strong human oversight.
